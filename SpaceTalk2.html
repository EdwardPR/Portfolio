SpaceTalk

Helping remote NASA personnel collaborate better by leveraging mobile virtual reality to bring their teams together in outer space.

Quick Overview

The NASA Jet Propulsion Lab proposed the challenge of designing an tool to aid scientists and engineers in the Europa Clipper mission to collaborate better by leveraging immersive technologies.

This is very open-ended design space, and our team set out to find out as much as we could about collaborative attitudes and past experiences from 13 engineers and scientists that would likely be working on the Europa mission, as well as 4 subject matter experts. This research provides the foundation for the rest of our project by giving us design principles to work with when we move into ideation and the refinement of our final prototype for the project.

What SpaceTalk Can Do

NASA mission planners frequently have trouble gathering feedback from other stakeholders on potential orbiter schedules. This primarily has to do with the difficulty in communicating complex information across distances. SpaceTalk leverages mobile virtual reality to make that an easy experience.

See the Orbiter Orientation

This is the zone you would see first when entering SpaceTalk.

Highlights:
  • See the orientation and bearing of the orbiter in relation to Europa, Earth, or the Sun
  • Travel in time along the trajectory to see how the orientation evolves

Discover Instrument Schedules

In the Schedule Zone, mission planners would be able to view exactly when which science instruments were operating and how.

Highlights:
  • See the different states of science instruments
  • Toggle science instruments to be visible or invisible

View Camera Coverage

In the inner-most information zone, the coverage of the different imaging instruments can be seen, so personnel can see exactly which areas of Europa are being captured.

Highlights:
  • Switch between a Wide Angle Camera (WAC), and Narrow Angle Camera (NAC)
  • See Europa from the point of view of the orbiter

Challenges We Overcame

This project challenged us in many different ways. However, the three challenges below were unique in their difficulty and scope.

Interviewees in Distant Cities

Remotely uncovering the nuances of NASA and the needs of the people who run it presented a huge challenge for us here. We overcame this by employing a few methods to elicit as much varied information as possible from our interviews.

Research Methods:
  • Semi-structured interview
  • Online closed card sort
  • Customized iterative workflow diagram that personnel could critique

Few Widely Accepted Design Guidelines

Because immersive experiences are so new, there are few heuristics that are widely accepted to reference. In response, we took inspiration from applications and games we found to be implemented well.

Inspiring Immersive Experiences:
  • Google Earth VR
  • Valve: The Lab
  • Virtual Virtual Reality

3D Prototyping is Time Intensive

Prototyping immersive experiences is time intensive, and the workflow is typically inefficient. For that reason, we tried to utilize the low threshold tools.

Best Prototyping Tool Found: A-Frame from the Mozilla Foundation

How We Arrived at Our Solution

We did not magically arrive out our solution. There was a long, systemic, process that we followed to develop SpaceTalk. It is detailed below.

Develop Design Principles

The first task we needed to do was develop a set of constraints, to limit our design space, and enable us to argue the merits of an idea.

Embrace NASA's Heritage

We must not only embrace NASA’s drive to push the envelope of what’s possible, but we must also prove the utility and robustness of our design through extensive testing and simulation.

Acknowledge the Politics

Acknowledge political motivations and tensions in an effort to improve collaboration between mission stakeholders.

Socially Translucent

Make system actions and activities clearly visible among all teams to instill trust and empathy.

Time Sensitive

Be cognizant of the limited time in the mission and take quick, time-sensitive, face-to-face interactions into account.

Emotionally Aware

Recognize the emotional valence of these types of missions in order to help users perform the right tasks in the right contexts.

Just Redundant Enough

Provide redundant data in all appropriate contexts, but only when necessary.

Adaptable and Flexible

Accommodate the expected uncertainty of orbiter missions and a constantly changing mission plan, schedule and timeline.

Immersive Where It Counts

Only employ 3D immersive environments when necessary. They can be amazing, but can also detract from productivity

Idea Generation

Now that we had a framework to work within, we needed to generate as many ideas as possible so we could sort and find a good idea to develop further.

From our research we had also identified three opportunity areas: Science Collaboration, science-engineer collaboration, and timeline visualization. We started with these to broadly ideate for.

  • Used Crazy 8's and Braiding
  • Generated 200+ ideas

Idea Selection

Through peer feedback and 2x2 matrices, we were able to narrow down to two compelling ideas. To help us determine which one to move forward with, we developed storyboards to understand their use cases.

In the end though, we decided to move forward with the Virtual Reality Timeline tool due to its feasibility and clear user needs, in contrast to Mixed Reality Sandbox.

Concept Prototype

We developed the VR Timeline idea more, adding features and visualizations ideas to fill the concept out more.

However, we were confronted again with the challenge that we could not test our idea in person with the people that would ostensibly use the product. In response, we developed two concept videos that allowed us to remotely demonstrate our idea in different use cases.

From the feedback we got, we realized we were not prepared to visualize scientific information in a trustworthy and feasible way. We pivoted and decided to focus entirely on the needs of mission planners how the need to solicit feedback on complex information remotely.

Information Architecture

Our first step in designing SpaceTalk was to build out the information architecture of our system. This provided the foundation that we drew from to design everythign else.

Highlights:
  • 5 different information zones with different experiences
  • People experience the information in the Jovian system, providing valuable context

Interaction Flow

We then established how someone might move through the system.

Highlights:
  • Either have a private or two person session
  • VR experience mediated through your personal phone
  • Simple menu system to minimize information searching

Headset Prototype

We were forced to make many assumption in the course of designing SpaceTalk due to the lack of widely-accepted design heuristics. We developed this headset prototype in order to test some the UI placement decisions we made were valid.

Why so high-fidelity?

A person's impression of a UI is greatly influenced by the environment they experience it in. Because we were proposing to place people into the Jovian system, we did not think lower fidelity prototypes with cardboard and paper would elicit good quality feedback.
